How do such things happen?

At No Gun Ri in Korea, American-led forces slaughtered hundreds of refugees fleeing North Korea and the battlefield after pinning them down under a bridge.  The killing went on for three days, and only ended when North Korean advances forced the Americans to withdraw from the area.

At Mỹ Lai in Vietnam, American forces killed hundreds of unarmed civilians, including elders and children.

Abu Ghraib

These events seem to have two things broadly in common:

  1. Occupying ground troops distrust local civilian populations.  They see them as part of hostile forces.  Or troops begin having trouble distinguishing between legitimate military targets and civilians.
  2. Command condones or ignores breaches of the rules of engagement, or obfuscates the rules of engagement.  In the case of No Gun Ri, leadership commanded ground forces to consider all persons advancing south as hostile.  In the case of Abu Ghraib, multiple leaders ordered and encouraged the criminal and inhumane treatment of prisoners there.

This is not even Russia's first set of war crimes.  They systematically bombed hospitals in Syria.  The United States bombed a hospital in Afghanistan, under quite different circumstances.

Comparing this to relatively isolated incidents rather than systematic ethnic cleansing and genocides implies that this is an isolated incident, or misstep, and not part of a wider Russian campaign of genocide.  But so far—and the situation evolves, making analyses obsolete every day—we have only seen one instance of such outright slaughter.  As the truth unfurls and more evidence becomes available, we may need to swap out our historical lens for a more grave one.  Furthermore, it does not align with Russia's strategic objectives, either as stated or as widely understood.  This kind of indiscriminate killing will make occupation more difficult, will harden international opposition, and may offend otherwise ambivalent Russians and Ukrainian Russian-speakers.  If Putin or his lieutenants ordered or condoned these atrocities, it represents yet another miscalculation.

—Lucas
